1. What Are Cookies?

Cookies are small text files that are placed on your computer or mobile device when you visit a website. They are widely used to make websites work more efficiently, as well as to provide reporting information. Cookies allow a website to recognize your device and remember information about your visit, such as your preferences or login details. This recognition can significantly improve your browsing experience by making interactions faster and more personalized. They can be temporary (session cookies) or persistent (persistent cookies), remaining on your device for a set period or until manually deleted. The data stored within cookies is typically non-personally identifiable, though in some cases, it can be linked to personal information you have provided. Understanding the fundamental nature of cookies is the first step in comprehending their role in modern web browsing. These small files are integral to the functionality of many online services, enabling features ranging from remembering items in a shopping cart to providing tailored content recommendations.

By using cookies, we aim to provide a seamless, efficient, and personalized browsing experience, tailoring our services to better meet your needs. Our use of cookies is always in compliance with applicable data protection laws and regulations, prioritizing your privacy and data security.

3. Types of Cookies We Use: Essential Cookies

Essential cookies are fundamental to the operation of our website. Without these cookies, certain core functionalities, such as navigating between pages, accessing secure areas, or remembering items in a shopping cart, would not be possible. They enable basic features like session management, which keeps you logged in as you browse, and security functions that protect against fraudulent activities. These cookies do not collect any personally identifiable information about you and are typically deleted when you close your browser (session cookies). Their primary purpose is to facilitate the transmission of communications over an electronic communications network, or to enable the provider of an information society service to provide a service explicitly requested by the user. Because they are strictly necessary for the website to function, your consent is not typically required for their use. Disabling these cookies would severely impair the usability of the website, potentially rendering certain features inaccessible or non-functional. They are vital for ensuring a stable and secure browsing environment.

4. Types of Cookies We Use: Analytical/Performance Cookies

Analytical or performance cookies collect information about how visitors use our website, such as which pages are visited most often, how long users spend on each page, and if they encounter any error messages. This data is aggregated and anonymized, meaning it cannot be used to identify individual users. We use this information to understand user behavior patterns, identify areas for improvement, and optimize the website's performance. For example, if we notice that a particular page has a high bounce rate, we can investigate and make necessary adjustments to improve its content or layout. These cookies help us measure the effectiveness of our content and marketing campaigns, allowing us to make data-driven decisions to enhance the overall user experience. They are crucial for continuous improvement and ensuring our website remains relevant and efficient for all visitors. While not strictly necessary for basic functionality, they provide invaluable insights for refining our services.

5. Types of Cookies We Use: Functionality Cookies

Functionality cookies allow our website to remember choices you make and provide enhanced, more personalized features. For instance, these cookies can remember your username, language preference, or the region you are in. They can also be used to remember changes you have made to text size, fonts, and other customizable parts of web pages. The information these cookies collect may be anonymized and they cannot track your browsing activity on other websites. Their purpose is to improve your experience by making it more convenient and tailored to your preferences, avoiding the need for you to repeatedly set your preferences. For example, if you set your preferred language, a functionality cookie will remember this choice for future visits. While not essential for the website's core operation, they significantly contribute to a more user-friendly and enjoyable browsing experience, making your interactions with our site smoother and more efficient.

6. Types of Cookies We Use: Targeting/Advertising Cookies

Targeting or advertising cookies are used to deliver advertisements that are more relevant to you and your interests. They are often placed by third-party advertising networks with our permission. These cookies remember that you have visited a website and this information may be shared with other organizations, such as advertisers. This allows us to display ads that are tailored to your browsing habits and preferences, both on our site and on other websites you visit. They also help us measure the effectiveness of our advertising campaigns and limit the number of times you see a particular advertisement. You have the option to control or opt-out of these cookies through your browser settings or specific opt-out mechanisms provided by advertising networks. Their use is typically based on your consent, which you can manage through our cookie consent banner.

7. First-Party vs. Third-Party Cookies

Cookies can be categorized as either first-party or third-party, depending on who sets them. First-party cookies are set directly by the website you are visiting, which in this case is Urban Beekeeping. These cookies are generally used for essential functions like maintaining your session, remembering your preferences, or gathering anonymous analytics about site usage. They are typically seen as less intrusive as they are controlled by the website you are directly interacting with. Third-party cookies, on the other hand, are set by a domain other than the one you are visiting. These are often used for cross-site tracking, advertising, and social media integration. For example, if we embed a social media feed on our site, the social media platform might set its own cookies. While third-party cookies provide useful functionalities like personalized ads or social sharing, they also raise more privacy concerns due to their ability to track your activity across multiple websites. We strive to limit third-party cookies to reputable partners and provide clear options for managing them.

8. Session Cookies vs. Persistent Cookies

Cookies also differ in their lifespan. Session cookies are temporary and are only stored on your device for the duration of your browsing session. They are deleted automatically once you close your web browser. These cookies are primarily used for essential functions, such as remembering items in your shopping cart or maintaining your login state as you navigate through a website. They ensure a smooth and continuous experience without requiring you to re-enter information repeatedly within the same session. Persistent cookies, conversely, remain on your device for a longer, predefined period or until you manually delete them. This allows websites to remember your preferences or login details for future visits, eliminating the need to reconfigure settings each time you return. They are often used for functionality, performance, and targeting purposes. The duration for which a persistent cookie remains on your device can vary from a few minutes to several years, depending on its purpose and the website's configuration. Understanding this distinction helps in managing your cookie preferences effectively.

Managing your cookie preferences empowers you to control your online privacy and tailor your browsing experience to your comfort level. 10. Managing Cookies in Google Chrome

To manage cookies in Google Chrome, open your browser and click on the three vertical dots (Menu) in the top-right corner. Navigate to "Settings," then scroll down and click on "Privacy and security" in the left sidebar. Under this section, select "Site Settings," and then click on "Cookies and site data." Here, you will find various options to control cookies. You can choose to "Allow all cookies," "Block all cookies (not recommended)," "Block third-party cookies in Incognito," or "Block third-party cookies." You also have the option to "See all site data and permissions," which allows you to view and delete specific cookies stored by individual websites. Furthermore, you can add sites to "Always clear cookies when windows are closed" or "Sites that can always use cookies." These granular controls provide significant flexibility in managing your cookie preferences within Chrome, allowing you to customize your browsing experience according to your privacy needs and preferences.

11. Managing Cookies in Mozilla Firefox

For Mozilla Firefox users, managing cookies is straightforward. Open Firefox and click on the three horizontal lines (Menu) in the top-right corner. Select "Settings," then navigate to "Privacy & Security" from the left-hand menu. In the "Enhanced Tracking Protection" section, you can choose from different levels of protection: "Standard," "Strict," or "Custom." "Standard" blocks known third-party tracking cookies, "Strict" blocks a wider range of trackers, and "Custom" allows you to manually select what to block, including cookies. Under the "Cookies and Site Data" section, you can click "Manage Data" to view and remove cookies from specific websites. You also have the option to "Delete cookies and site data when Firefox is closed." These settings give you comprehensive control over how Firefox handles cookies, enabling you to balance privacy with website functionality. Firefox is known for its strong privacy features, and these cookie management options are a testament to that commitment.

12. Managing Cookies in Apple Safari

If you are using Apple Safari, you can manage your cookie settings by opening Safari and going to "Safari" in the top menu bar, then selecting "Preferences." Click on the "Privacy" tab. Here, you will find options to control how Safari handles cookies and website data. You can select "Prevent cross-site tracking" to block third-party cookies that track you across websites. There is also an option to "Block all cookies," which will prevent all websites from storing cookies on your device, though this is generally not recommended as it can break many website functionalities. You can also click "Manage Website Data..." to see a list of websites that have stored data on your device, including cookies, and remove specific ones. Safari's privacy features are designed to give users more control over their data, and these cookie management options reflect that commitment, providing a user-friendly interface for managing your online privacy.

13. Managing Cookies in Microsoft Edge

For users of Microsoft Edge, managing cookies is accessible through the browser settings. Open Edge and click on the three horizontal dots (Settings and more) in the top-right corner. Select "Settings," then navigate to "Privacy, search, and services" from the left-hand menu. Under the "Tracking prevention" section, you can choose from "Basic," "Balanced," or "Strict" levels of tracking prevention, which includes blocking various types of cookies. Further down, under "Clear browsing data," you can click "Choose what to clear" to delete specific types of data, including cookies and other site data. Additionally, under "Cookies and site permissions," you can click "Manage and delete cookies and site data" to view and remove cookies from individual websites, or choose to "Block third-party cookies." Edge provides a robust set of tools to help you manage your online privacy and control how cookies are used during your browsing sessions.

14. The Purpose of Essential Cookies Explained

Essential cookies, also known as strictly necessary cookies, are fundamental to the basic operation of any website. This includes, but is not limited to, enabling user navigation, allowing access to secure areas of the website, and remembering information as you move from one page to another within a single browsing session. Similarly, if you log into an account, an essential cookie maintains your authenticated state, preventing you from having to log in on every new page. These cookies are crucial for providing the service explicitly requested by the user and are exempt from consent requirements in many privacy regulations because their absence would render the website unusable for its intended purpose. They are temporary, session-based, and do not store any personal data.

For example, if a specific page has a high exit rate, analytical data can prompt us to investigate why users are leaving that page. By continuously analyzing this data, we can make informed decisions to enhance content, streamline user journeys, and ultimately provide a more effective and enjoyable website experience.

22. Cookie Lifespan and Expiration

The lifespan of a cookie refers to how long it remains stored on your device before it expires. As discussed, session cookies are temporary and are deleted once you close your browser. Persistent cookies, however, have a predefined expiration date set by the website or the third-party service that placed them. This expiration can range from a few minutes to several years. For example, a persistent cookie used to remember your language preference might be set to expire in one year, while a cookie used for advertising might have a longer lifespan to track long-term interests. The expiration date is determined by the cookie creator based on its purpose. Once a cookie expires, it is automatically removed from your device. You can also manually clear cookies from your browser at any time, effectively overriding their set expiration dates. Understanding cookie lifespans helps you manage your digital footprint and privacy more effectively.

28. The Concept of "Do Not Track" (DNT) Signals

"Do Not Track" (DNT) is a privacy preference that users can set in their web browsers to signal to websites that they do not want their online activities to be tracked. When enabled, the browser sends a DNT signal to the websites you visit. However, it's important to note that DNT is a voluntary standard, and websites are not legally obligated to honor it. The effectiveness of DNT depends on whether websites choose to respect the signal. Currently, there is no universally adopted legal or technical standard for how websites should respond to DNT signals. As such, while we respect privacy, our website may not fully respond to DNT signals at this time. We encourage users to manage their cookie preferences directly through our consent banner and browser settings for more definitive control over their data. The DNT initiative highlights the ongoing efforts to provide users with more control over their online privacy, even as the industry works towards a unified approach.

36. Web Beacons and Pixel Tags: Related Technologies

In contrast to cookies, which are stored on a user’s computer hard drive, web beacons are embedded invisibly on web pages or in emails. They help us determine whether users have opened emails or visited certain pages, providing insights into the effectiveness of our marketing campaigns and content. While web beacons do not store information on your device in the same way cookies do, they can work in conjunction with cookies to gather data. We use these technologies to understand aggregated user behavior and improve our services, always with a commitment to privacy and data security.
