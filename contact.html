Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need special permits to keep bees in my city?

Regulations for urban beekeeping vary significantly by city, municipality, and even neighborhood associations. It is crucial to research and comply with all local ordinances, zoning laws, and registration requirements before setting up a hive. Many cities require permits or registration, and some may have specific rules regarding hive placement, density, and signage. We strongly advise contacting your local government's planning department, agricultural department, or a local beekeeping association to get the most accurate and up-to-date information for your specific location.

How much space do I need for an urban beehive?

While bees don't require a vast amount of space, you need enough room for the hive itself, plus working space for you to conduct inspections safely. A typical Langstroth hive takes up about 2x2 feet of ground space, but you'll need at least 3-4 feet of clear space around it for comfortable access. Rooftops, balconies, and secluded backyard corners can be suitable. Consideration should also be given to the bees' flight path, ideally directing them upwards and away from pedestrian areas.

Is urban beekeeping safe for my neighbors and pets?

With proper management and hive placement, urban beekeeping can be very safe. Bees are generally docile when foraging and only become defensive near their hive if they feel threatened. Educating your neighbors about your bees, ensuring the hive entrance is directed away from common areas, and providing a water source can significantly minimize any issues. For individuals with severe bee sting allergies, extra precautions and communication are necessary. We provide guidance on responsible beekeeping practices to ensure harmony with your surroundings.

What kind of honey yield can I expect from an urban hive?

Honey yield can vary greatly depending on factors such as the strength of your colony, the availability of nectar sources in your urban area, and local weather conditions. A healthy, established urban hive can produce anywhere from 20 to 60 pounds of honey per year, sometimes more. New colonies will focus on building comb and brood, so their first-year yield might be lower. The unique floral diversity of urban environments often results in distinct and delicious honey flavors.
